Measures of centre

A measure of centre locates where the data cluster. Three are in common use:

MeasureDefinitionNotationWhen it works best
Mean Sum of all values divided by count. $\bar{x}$ (sample), $\mu$ (population) Continuous data, no extreme outliers.
Median The middle value when data are ordered. $\tilde{x}$ or $M$ Skewed data or when outliers are present.
Mode The most frequently occurring value. — Categorical data; multimodal distributions.

The mean in detail

The sample mean sums all observations and divides by $n$:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i$$

The population mean uses the same formula over all $N$ members, giving the true parameter $\mu$. In practice $\mu$ is unknown — $\bar{x}$ is our estimate of it.

Worked Example

Seven patients' hospital stays (days): 3, 5, 5, 6, 7, 9, 22.

MeasureCalculationResult
Mean $(3+5+5+6+7+9+22) \div 7$ 8.1 days
Median Middle value of ordered set 6 days
Mode Most frequent value 5 days

The 22-day outlier pulls the mean to 8.1, well above most of the data. The median (6) is a more representative centre here. This is why house prices and salaries are reported as medians — a few extreme values would distort the mean.

Measures of spread

Centre alone is not enough. Two datasets can share the same mean but look completely different. Spread quantifies how much the data vary around the centre.

Range and IQR

MeasureDefinitionWeakness
Range Max $-$ Min Dominated by a single outlier.
IQR $Q_3 - Q_1$ — spread of the middle 50%. Ignores the tails entirely.

The IQR is resistant to outliers for the same reason the median is — it only looks at the middle of the data. It is the basis of the box plot.

Variance and standard deviation

Variance measures average squared deviation from the mean. Squaring makes all deviations positive and penalises large departures more heavily.

LevelMeasureFormulaNotation
Population Variance $\sigma^2 = \dfrac{1}{N}\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i - \mu)^2$ $\sigma^2$
Std deviation $\sqrt{\sigma^2}$ $\sigma$
Sample Variance $s^2 = \dfrac{1}{n-1}\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2$ $s^2$
Std deviation $\sqrt{s^2}$ $s$

The standard deviation is in the same units as the original data, making it interpretable. Variance is in squared units — useful mathematically, harder to communicate directly.

Why $n-1$ and not $n$?

Dividing by $n$ when estimating from a sample consistently underestimates the true spread — the sample clusters around its own mean more tightly than the population does. Dividing by $n-1$ corrects for this. This is called Bessel's correction. When $n$ is large the difference is trivial; it matters most in small samples.

Shape

Knowing centre and spread still leaves the question of how the data are distributed. Shape describes the pattern.

Skewness

Skewness describes the asymmetry of a distribution.

TypeWhat it looks likeMean vs MedianCommon examples
Symmetric Mirror image left and right. Mean $\approx$ Median Heights, measurement error
Right-skewed (positive) Long tail to the right. Mean $>$ Median Salaries, house prices, hospital stays
Left-skewed (negative) Long tail to the left. Mean $<$ Median Age at retirement, exam scores near ceiling

The hospital stays example is right-skewed — the 22-day outlier pulls the mean rightward past the median. The mean chases the tail; the median stays near the bulk of the data.

Kurtosis

Kurtosis measures the weight of the tails relative to a normal distribution. High kurtosis means extreme values are less rare than you would expect. It matters more in later chapters; for now, just know it exists.
