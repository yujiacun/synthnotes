The two pillars

Statistics splits into two branches:

BranchWhat it doesExamples
Descriptive Summarise and organise data so patterns are visible. means, percentages, charts, tables
Inferential Use a sample to draw conclusions about the population it came from. hypothesis tests, confidence intervals, regression

Descriptive describes what happened. Inferential generalises beyond it.

Worked Example

A teacher records test scores for her 30 students:

MeasureValue
Mean 72.4
Median 74
Min 41
Max 98

Descriptive: mean 72.4, range 41–98 — these 30 students.

Inferential: to claim something about all students taught this way, build a confidence interval like "95% confident the true mean is between 68.1 and 76.7."

CIs are inferential — and why 95%?

A confidence interval looks descriptive — it is just a range. But it is inferential: it uses sample data to make a probability statement about an unknown population parameter. The interval reaches beyond what you observed.

The 95% level is convention, not law. Ronald Fisher's informal $p < 0.05$ threshold from the 1920s–30s set the standard; a 95% CI is the interval form of that same idea. Other fields set their own bars:

FieldTypical levelReason
Most social / medical research95%Convention (Fisher)
Engineering / safety-critical99–99.9%High cost of error
Exploratory analysis90%Less stringent threshold acceptable
Particle physics99.9999% ($5\sigma$)Extremely high bar for a discovery claim

Choose your confidence level based on the consequences of being wrong. 95% is the default only because everyone else uses it.

Key vocabulary

LevelTermWhat it isNotation
Population Population The complete set of items you care about (every UK adult, every die roll, every widget produced by a factory). $N$ (size)
Parameter A number that describes the population — usually unknown. Greek: $\mu$, $\sigma$, $\rho$
Sample Sample A subset of the population that you actually observe. $n$ (size)
Statistic A number computed from the sample — used to estimate a parameter. Roman: $\bar{x}$, $s$, $r$

The whole game of inferential statistics is using statistics to estimate parameters.

Worked Example — UK student screen time

You want to know the average daily screen time of UK university students.

LevelTermIn this exampleNotation
Population Population All UK uni students ($\approx 2.9$ million). $N \approx 2.9\text{M}$
Parameter True mean screen time — unknown. $\mu$ (unknown)
Sample Sample 400 students across 10 universities. $n = 400$
Statistic Mean of your 400: 4.7 hrs/day. Your best estimate of $\mu$. $\bar{x} = 4.7$

Inferential statistics is about quantifying how close 4.7 is likely to be to the true value.

Types of data

The type of a variable determines which statistical tools apply to it.

ClassTypeDescriptionExamples
Categorical Nominal Unordered categories. blood type, colour, country
Ordinal Ordered categories with unequal gaps. satisfaction rating, education level, shirt size
Numerical Discrete Countable values, usually integers. number of siblings, goals scored, emails received
Continuous Any value on a range. height, weight, temperature, time

You can average weights, not postcodes. You can rank shirt sizes, but you can't subtract M from XL. The type tells you which operations and charts are valid.

Questions

  1. A hospital records the average recovery time for all 200 patients treated last month. Is this descriptive or inferential statistics? Explain why.
  2. A polling company surveys 1,000 UK adults and finds 54% support a new policy. Identify the population, sample, parameter, and statistic. What notation would you use for each?
  3. A researcher records the following about each participant: age, satisfaction rating (1–5), number of hospital visits last year, and blood type. Classify each variable by type.
  4. A 95% CI for mean daily steps is (7,200 – 8,400). Your colleague says "that's just a description of the range we observed." What's wrong with that?
  5. A clinical drug trial uses a 99% CI rather than the standard 95%. What does this choice tell you about how the researchers view the consequences of being wrong?
